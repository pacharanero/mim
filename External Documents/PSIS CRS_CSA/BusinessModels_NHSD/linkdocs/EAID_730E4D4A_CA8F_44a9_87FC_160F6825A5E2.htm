Ian Hanson – ( an adult with left arm pain).

Ian is a 64 year old male who calls NHS Direct as he has been experiencing some pain in his left arm.  He reaches Pippa, who a Health Advisor.  Pippa takes details of the number Ian is calling from and other information about his location.  Ian is currently calling from work and he explains that he is calling because he wants some advice about what he can take for a painful arm.  He says he has had the pain in the top of his arm near to his left shoulder off and on for the past couple of days but it has been worse today.  He says he is on his own so has no one else to ask.

Pippa assesses Ian using the NHS Direct call streaming and prioritisation tool and establishes that Ian does not have pain at present but has had pain several times within the last 1-2 hours lasting for 10-15 minutes each time.  He has no other symptoms and says that he does not have heart trouble.  A priority 2 is assigned to the call and after completing the rest of the demographic information and identifying the name and surgery details of Ian’s GP, Pippa places the call on a queue for a nurse advisor to call back for a nurse assessment.  Pippa advises Ian to call back in the event of his symptoms becoming worse.

15 minutes later, Mary, a nurse advisor, picks up the call from the queue and calls Ian on the number given in the record.   Mary takes a history from Ian including any medication he is currently taking, any significant conditions he suffers from and any known allergies he has.  Ian indicates he is taking some medication as a result of previous chest pains but he cant remember the name of the tablet.  Mary asks Ian’s permission to review the information held in his Summary Care Record.  Ian agrees and she discovers that he is taking Pantoprazole.  She then takes Ian through a full assessment asking questions from prompts providing by the assessment system.  At the end of this process Mary advises Ian that he should make an appointment in the next 6 hours at his GP surgery for further advice.

Mary also advises Ian that the symptoms described during this call suggest that he should contact the GP practice today.  She also advises Ian:

- if you are unable to contact the practice, please call back

- Avoid strenuous activity

- WORSENING: If symptoms persist, worsen or any new symptoms develop, call us back 

Mary records this advice given in the system and concludes the call with a disposition of ‘GP 6 hours’. 
June Harrison  – (requesting Health Information but also needs a symptom based assessment).

June is a 54 year old lady who calls NHS Direct as she is requesting information about Osteoarthritis.  She has been having some knee pain and thinks it could be osteoarthritis and would like some leaflets sending out.

June reaches Anne, who a Health Advisor.  Anne takes details of the number June is calling from and other information about her location.  June explains that she is calling because she wants some advice about Osteoarthritis. 

Anne assesses June using the NHS Direct call streaming and prioritisation tool and establishes that June is calling for Health Information and can be transferred to the Health Information Queue for a call back from an advisor.

A couple of hours later Fiona, a Health Information advisor, picks up the call from the queue and calls June on the number given in the record.   Fiona establishes that June wants some information about Osteoarthritis.  Fiona discusses what specific information June would like and identifies that some leaflets held in the library would be suitable. During her conversation with June she finds out that June has got knee pain and June confesses that she has not seen a healthcare professional about this problem.  Fiona advises June that she could speak to one of the nurses, in confidence, and June agrees to speak to a nurse.   Fiona finishes dealing with June’s information request and places the call on the queue after advising June that a nurse will call her back in around 1 hour.

55 minutes later June is called by Jean, nurse advisor, who asks June about her knee pain.  June has never discussed this with a healthcare professional before and Jean undertakes a full assessment with June.  At the end of this Jean recommends that:

The symptoms described during this call suggest that you should discuss them with the GP practice when the surgery is next open

- if you are unable to contact the practice, please call back

- Apply cold compress to area to address pain and swelling

- Avoid activities that cause pain swelling or discomfort

- elevate the affected area above the level of the heart especially when sitting or lying down (put the affected area on pillows if appropriate) 

- the pharmacist can advice on appropriate short term treatment of symptoms

- WORSENING:If symptoms persist, worsen or any new symptoms develop, call us back 

Jean records this advice given in the system and concludes the call with a disposition of ‘Advised to Contact Primary Care Service Next Working Day’. 
Michael Turner – ( a child with diahorrea)

Michael is 16 months old.  He is normally fit and well but today he has passed a watery stool in his nappy and his Mum, Trudi, is concerned.

Trudi decides to call NHS Direct for some advice and she gets through to Janet who is a Health Advisor.   Janet assesses Michael using the NHS Direct call streaming and prioritisation tool and establishes that Michael seems well at present and he has no other symptoms.  A priority 2 is assigned to the call and after completing the rest of the demographic information and identifying the name and surgery details of Michael’s GP, Janet places the call on a queue for a nurse advisor to call back for a nurse assessment. Janet advises Trudi to call back if Michael seems to become worse.

20 minutes later, Lindsey, a nurse advisor, picks up the call from the queue and calls Trudi on the number given in the record.   Lindsey then takes Trudi through a full assessment asking questions from prompts provided by the assessment system.  Other than the loose stool Michael appears well with no vomiting or raised temperature.  At the end of this process Lindsey advises Trudi that Michael does not need to see anyone today and that Trudi can care for him at home.  She offers some care advice for Trudi.

Lindsey advises Trudi that

The symptoms described during this call suggest that the problem concerned can be managed at home
-       If symptoms persist we recommend that you discuss the symptoms with your GP
-       Diarrhoea is common and usually resolves in less than one week
-       There is no evidence that fasting will have any benefit
-       Increased fluid intake and changes to the diet are the best treatment for diarrhoea to replace fluid losses
-       Include fruit juices, soups and foods high in carbohydrate, such as bread, pasta, potatoes or rice
-       Oral rehydration fluids from the pharmacist help to replace lost sugars and salt preventing dehydration
-       WORSENING :If symptoms persist, worsen or any new symptoms develop, call us back


Mary records this advice given in the system and concludes the call with a disposition of ‘Self Care’. 

Sally Basford – (36 year old pregnant female with heavy vaginal bleeding)

Sally (36yrs) is 16 weeks pregnant.  Her husband is calling on her behalf and the call is taken by Katy who is a Health Advisor.  Katy takes the number John Basford is calling from, John gives her a mobile telephone number and Katy then collects information about Sally’s location.  Katy establishes that John is calling because Sally is 16 weeks pregnant and has some vaginal bleeding which started this evening.

Katy uses the NHS Direct call streaming and prioritisation tool to assess the priority of Sally’s symptoms and reaches a priority 1. She collects the rest of Sally’s demographic details.

As a priority 1 call, Sally’s call is warm transferred to (passed immediately to) a Nurse Advisor (Sue).  Sue asks if she can speak to Sally personally and Sally agrees. Sue carries out a clinical assessment using the NHS Direct Pregnancy - vaginal bleeding algorithm.  Sally is not shocked and the bleeding has not followed either abdominal or vaginal trauma but she is passing some clots per vagina and has some lower abdominal pain at present.  She has not had antenatal bloods done yet and is not sure what her blood group is although she did have a blood transfusion 2 years ago because she had an abnormal pregnancy which was terminated. She can’t remember what the type of pregnancy was but say is was a long word like “mole or something”.

Sue asks Sally’s permission to view her Summary Care Record which shows that Sally had a Hydatidiform Mole in 2005 and her blood group is noted a B Rh negative.

Sue concludes the assessment and advises Sally :

The symptoms described during this call suggest that you should contact the GP practice today

- if you are unable to contact the practice, please call back

- Avoid strenuous activity

- WORSENING: If symptoms persist, worsen or any new symptoms develop, call us back 

She concludes the call with a disposition of Refer to Primary Care Urgent.
